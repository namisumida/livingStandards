Standards of Living

Using data from the World Bank, I compared several health metrics in the US and in some of the least developed countries in the world to better understand how standards of living can widely vary around the world.

By Nami Sumida
March 2019

As of 2016, the average person can expect to live to 72 years old. But people in many countries can expect to live much longer. Those in Hong Kong, Japan, and Macau have an average life expectancy of 84. On the other hand, several have a life expectancy of much less: 15 countries are below 60.

Since 1960, the global average has increased by nearly 10 years - from 63 years old to 72. And while life expectancy ranges from country-to-country, all have seen increases in the past 50 years. In some countries, like Bhutan and the Maldives, increases are large (40 years!), while in some Eastern Europe countries like Ukraine, Lithuania, and Latvia, increases are under 5 years.

Let's pick out 20 random countries and see how life expectancy has increased in each.

And while life expectancy has increased in all countries, there is still a large gap between the most and least developed countries.

Take the U.S. for example. The U.S. has one of the highest life expectancies: 79. In comparison, people in some of the least developed countries, like Sudan, Tanzania, and Myanmar, tend to live to 63. That's lower than life expectancy in the U.S. in 1960.

What are "least developed countries (LDC's)"?

This is a categorization by the UN. Using a wide range of key economic, health, and education indicators, the Committee for Development Policy creates a list of least developed countries, made up of "low-income countries confronting severe structural impediments to sustainable development". These countries tend to have economic and environmental vulnerabilities and long-term structural handicaps.

Similar patterns are found when it comes to other metrics, like % of undernourished population, under 5 and maternal mortality rates, and access to sanitation services and electricity. The progress seen in these least developed countries in the past several years is remarkable - much more progress than seen in the U.S. However, these countries still lag behind the U.S. in all of these metrics - both on average and individually.
