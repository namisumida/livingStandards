Standards of Living

Using data from the World Bank, we compared several health metrics in the US and in some of the least developed countries in the world to better understand how standards of living can widely vary around the world.

By Nami Sumida
April 2019
LIFE EXPECTANCY

Defined as "the average number of years a newsborn is expected to live if mortality patterns at the time of birth remains constant in the future", life expectancy across the world is at 72. In other words, the average person on Earth can expect to live to 72 years old. That's up from 63 in the 1980s. But life expectancy of people in the U.S. is higher, currently at around 79.

In comparison, life expectancy of people in some of the least developed countries is much lower - at 63. That's lower than life expectancy in the U.S. over 50 years ago.

What are "least developed countries"?

This is a categorization by the United Nations. Using a wide range of key economic, health, and education indicators, the Committee for Development Policy creates a list of least developed countries (LDCs), made up of "low-income countries confronting severe structural impediments to sustainable development". They tend to have economic and environmental vulnerabilities and long-term structural handicaps. (Read more on their page.)
