Standards of Living

Using data from the World Bank, we compared several health metrics in the US and in some of the least developed countries in the world to better understand how standards of living can widely vary around the world.

By Nami Sumida
April 2019
LIFE EXPECTANCY

As of 2016, the average person on Earth can expect to live to 72 years old. But people in many countries can expect to live much longer. Those in Hong Kong, Japan, and Macau have an average life expectancy of 84. On the other hand, several have a life expectancy of much less: 15 countries are below 60.

Since 1960, the global average has increased by nearly 10 years - from 63 years old to 72. And while life expectancy ranges from country-to-country, all have seen increases in the past 50 years. In some countries, like Bhutan and the Maldives, increases are large (40 years!), while in some Eastern Europe countries like Ukraine, Lithuania, and Latvia, increases are under 5 years.

Let's pick out 20 random countries and see how life expectancy has increased in each.

And while life expectancy has increased in all countries, there is still a large gap between the most and least developed countries.

The U.S. has one of the highest life expectancies: 79. In comparison, people in some of the least developed countries tend to live to 63. That's lower than life expectancy in the U.S. in 1960.

What are "least developed countries"?

This is a categorization by the United Nations. Using a wide range of key economic, health, and education indicators, the Committee for Development Policy creates a list of least developed countries, made up of "low-income countries confronting severe structural impediments to sustainable development". They tend to have economic and environmental vulnerabilities and long-term structural handicaps. (Read more on their page.)
